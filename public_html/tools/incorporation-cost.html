How the math works

Every incorporation has three cost layers:

  1. Government filing fee - non-negotiable. Set by the jurisdiction.
  2. Name search (NUANS or provincial Name Request) - only if you want a named company.
  3. Service fee - what you pay an online incorporator or lawyer for the work.

DIY incorporation is just government fee + name search. Services bundle the filing, minute book, and post-incorporation paperwork for $200–$800. Lawyers run $1,000–$3,000 and are worth it for complex share structures, co-founder splits, or holdco setups.

What “service” covers

An online incorporator typically includes: the NUANS / name search, Articles of Incorporation filing, organizational resolutions, share certificates, a digital minute book, and CRA business number registration. Some include a year of registered office service. The big three for Canadian small businesses:

  • Ownr - the most polished UX. RBC-owned. Strong for ON, BC, AB, QC, federal.
  • LawDepot - budget-friendly for federal, Ontario, Alberta, BC, and Saskatchewan, with add-ons only if you need them.
  • MyCorporation - Canada-specific filing support with bylaws and sample corporate documents in the package.

FAQ

Why is Alberta cheaper but has an extra fee?

Alberta uses private registry agents instead of a government portal. The filing fee is lower, but the agent adds $50–$200 to process it on your behalf.

What’s the cheapest legal way?

A numbered federal corporation: $200 filing fee, no NUANS needed. Add a CRA business number (free), open a Wealthsimple Business account (free), and you’re operating for $200.

Does this include ongoing costs?

No - this estimator covers one-time setup. Plan for $300–$2,000/year in accounting, annual return filings, and software.
